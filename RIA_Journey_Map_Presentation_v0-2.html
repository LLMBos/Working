1.1 · The mission

Lose the operational drag.
Return time to the work only a human can do.

The advisor wears three hats and runs between a dozen disconnected tools. The platform collapses the operational load so two kinds of time come back — open time for growth and learning, enhanced time for deeper client work — while a small slice stays reserved for advisor oversight and approvals, because the RIA is always in control.

Three roles.

Behind every role — the data problem
The messiness of today's tech stack comes down to this: the data the advisor needs is scattered across custodians, planning tools, CRMs, and spreadsheets. Just getting to it, and trusting it, is most of the work — no matter which hat the advisor has on at that moment.
1.4 · Pain points

Five places where the day
works against the advisor.

Each category represents a place where today's tools and workflows actively cost hours, weaken relationships, or put the practice itself at risk.

01
Administrative drag
3+ hours of prep per client per quarter. Notes and CRM upkeep happen after-hours, days late.
02
Tool fragmentation
Same client data entered across 3–5 tools. 30–60 min lost per workflow to switching.
03
Onboarding friction
10–30 days to transfer. Days 10–20 are peak client anxiety and peak advisor silence.
04
Lots of data, little insight
Portfolio, market, client data in silos. Hours pulling reports, no time interpreting them.
05
BD & retention starve
BD loses to delivery every week. Quiet clients are detected too late.
Taken together, these five pressures add up to a practice running in reactive mode. Every moment in the opportunity map is a response to one of them.
1.5 · How might we

Four statements anchor the work.

Each HMW is intentionally wide — meant to spawn multiple feature concepts, not constrain them.

01
How might we lose the operational drag so both open time (BD, learning, growth) and enhanced time (deeper client work) return to the RIA?
02
How might we unify the fragmented tool stack into one seamless, dynamic workflow that feels like the right fit — neither overwhelming nor limiting?
03
How might we give the RIA X-ray clarity on clients and portfolios — with actionable insight and anticipation — while keeping judgment and advice firmly in the advisor's hands?
04
How might we make the advisor's own practice — book, business, skill, growth — as systematic as the client work it supports?
1.6 · Design principles

Tiebreakers when tradeoffs show up.

Principles that guide every design and product decision. When we have to choose, these are the rules.

1
The RIA is in control
The advisor is always the conductor. The platform suggests, drafts, prepares — the advisor decides.
2
Transparency over magic
Show sources. Show rationale. Show the work. Black boxes erode trust in a fiduciary context.
3
Seamless, not intrusive
Fades into the background when not needed. Surfaces with precision when it is. No jarring hand-offs.
4
Quality over quantity
Fewer, better-reasoned signals beat a flood of notifications. Earn attention by deserving it.
5
The human relationship is the product
Automation deepens human moments, never substitutes. The relationship is what the practice sells.

2.0 · How to read the journey map

Two tracks, one relationship,
three diagnostic layers.

Every phase runs the advisor and client in parallel. The gap between them is the intersection zone — where trust is made, tested, or lost. Every tension point falls into one of three diagnostic layers that tell you what kind of intervention to design.

Advisor track What the advisor does, how long it takes, and where the work gets heavy.
Client track What the client experiences, feels, and expects in the same moment.

Automation opportunity

Reduce manual overhead. Tasks that can be offloaded, templated, or executed automatically — freeing time and attention.

Agentic AI

Proactive sensing and action. The platform notices, synthesizes, or recommends without being asked — advisor decides whether to act.

Human / trust layer

Where the experience feels hard for a human, not a machine. Automation protects this layer; it does not replace it.

01
Phase 01 · First touchpoints → signed agreement

Prospecting

The advisor is selling — building credibility and proving fit. The client is evaluating — trying to decide whether to trust. Nothing is said aloud about either. The work happens on reputation, referrals, and the pitch meeting.

Advisor
01Qualify & pitch
Research the prospect, prepare the deck, schedule the intro call. 2–4 hours per prospect. Mostly unbillable.
02Build credibility
Follow up with supporting materials, tailored notes, and answer questions. Relationship-building without a signed agreement.
03Close
Walk through fees, advisory agreement, custodian choice. Nudge the yes. Start planning the handoff to onboarding.
Client
01Anxiety & evaluation
Who do I trust with my money? Googles the advisor, checks credentials, asks friends. Forms a first impression long before the meeting.
02The fit test
Does this person understand me? Am I being sold to? Watches for warmth, competence, and whether the pitch is about them or about the product.
03Quiet decision
Rarely says yes in the room. Goes home, talks to a spouse, re-reads the email. Decides on feel plus fact.
Intersection zone
Trust is forming
Advisor is selling. Client is evaluating. Neither says what they actually want. The advisor wants the close; the client wants to feel safe. The gap between the two is where pitches fail — and where a small, well-placed signal of preparation can make all the difference.

02
Phase 02 · Signed agreement → first month under management

Onboarding

Between the handshake and the first statement. Document collection, account opening, and the transfer window. Advisor activity is mostly paperwork and status checks; client emotion swings from relief to peak anxiety and back. This is the phase where the first impression of the relationship is permanently set.

Advisor
01Collect documents
Statements, tax returns, IDs, beneficiary forms, existing account details. Chase missing items. Explain why each is needed.
02Open and fund
Set up custodian accounts. Initiate transfers (ACATS and non-ACATS). Send DocuSign requests. Reconcile what actually arrives.
03Stay visible
Send periodic status updates — even when nothing has changed. Acknowledge the silence of the transfer window so the client doesn't read it as inactivity.
Client
01Relief & hope
Decision made. Starts gathering documents. Expects things to move quickly now that the yes is in.
02Peak anxiety during transfer
Balances look wrong. Old account appears empty. New account isn't funded yet. No phone call has come. Wonders if this was a mistake.
03Settling in
Accounts arrive. Logs in to the portal. First impression of the platform, statement format, communication cadence. Whatever happens here becomes the baseline.
Intersection zone
The silent transfer window
Days 10 to 20 are simultaneously peak client anxiety and peak advisor inactivity. There is nothing the advisor can legally do to move a transfer faster. The client interprets silence as neglect. Every subsequent review and referral opportunity gets discounted by how this window felt.

03
Phase 03 · Building the complete financial picture

Discovery & plan

The most under-designed phase in the entire relationship. Three simultaneous layers of work — data collection, narrative-building, and trust formation — compressed into a handful of conversations. Discovery stretches across 5–6 meetings in the first few months. Everything after depends on what gets captured here.

Advisor
01Discovery meetings
5–6 meetings, 90–120 min each, of open-ended conversation. Goals, fears, family dynamics, prior advisor history. The dual-brain problem — conversation, data, notes, body language at once.
02Build the full picture
Collect structured data (assets, liabilities, income, tax, estate, insurance) alongside semi-structured narrative (goals, timeline, values). Two incompatible capture modes, one client in the room.
03Model and present
Run cash flow, retirement projection, tax scenarios. Turn the picture into a proposal the client can understand and sign off on.
Client
01Hope & vulnerability
Shares retirement fears, family obligations, past regrets. Wants to feel heard before feeling analyzed.
02Document fatigue
Has been asked for the same statement three times by three systems. Feels judged by the state of their paperwork.
03Plan reveal
Sees the plan for the first time. Either feels relieved (someone has a map) or overwhelmed (too many numbers, not enough story).
Intersection zone
Vulnerability peak
Client shares real fears about money, family, and aging. Advisor must hold the emotional space and simultaneously capture structured data. This is where the trust layer is built — and where over-engineered capture tools actively get in the way.

Phase 04 · Quarterlies, drift, and the drift of memory

Ongoing relationship

The steady state — where the practice lives 90% of the time and where most advisor time quietly disappears. Quarterly prep, drift monitoring, and ad-hoc responses eat the hours promised to planning and growth. Clients increasingly expect on-demand service and a running sense of portfolio health between reviews.

Advisor
01Quarterly prep & review
Pull reports, draft agenda, review drift, prep talking points. A significant chunk of each week depending on book size.
02Ad-hoc responses
Inbound calls and emails — market news, one-off life questions, tax letters. Arrive unscheduled, block deep work.
03Monitor and adjust
Watch for drift, flag outliers, rebalance when needed. Largely invisible work the client assumes is always happening.
Client
01Passive trust
Trusts the advisor but doesn't fully understand the plan. Reads emails when convenient. Assumes silence means everything is fine.
02On-demand expectation
Wants answers when questions arise — not just at the next scheduled review. Expects a light, current view of portfolio health between meetings.
03Low-grade market anxiety
Reacts to headlines. Wonders whether anyone is watching. Wants a quick "yes, we see this, here's the context" without needing a phone call.
Intersection zone
Silent drift
Client forgets details. Advisor forgets context. Without active memory, the relationship coasts on goodwill until a quarterly review forces re-anchoring. The best designs here reduce the cost of remembering, not the frequency of review.

05
Phase 05 · When life happens — and speed equals trust

Life event response

Job changes, divorces, inheritances, medical diagnoses, home sales, deaths, births. These are the moments where a financial plan gets tested against reality. The hardest problem isn't speed — it's detection. Most life events are discovered when the client calls to report them, often weeks late.

Advisor
01Detect the signal
Usually learned via client call, not system. The highest risk of miss — and the highest emotional cost when the advisor finds out late.
02Re-model the plan
Rework cash flow, tax implications, insurance coverage, beneficiaries. Turn a life change into a revised plan quickly.
03Execute
Move money, adjust allocations, file paperwork, coordinate with accountant and attorney. Speed matters to the client; precision matters to compliance.
Client
01Disrupted & overwhelmed
Life just changed. Needs guidance fast. Doesn't know what questions to ask. Needs the advisor to ask them instead.
02Emotional load
Grief, fear, excitement, anger — depending on the event. Financial logic is a second-order concern until the first-order feeling is acknowledged.
03Urgency
Wants a response now. Doesn't know how long the advisor's queue is. Reads silence as abandonment.
Intersection zone
Urgency mismatch
Client needs a response now. Advisor is managing many other households. Speed equals trust — not because the answer has to be immediate, but because the acknowledgment has to be.

06
Phase 06 · Looking back, looking forward, deciding together

Review & growth

The annual punctuation of the relationship. For the advisor, it's the moment to synthesize the year and set up the next. For the client, it's the moment to decide — consciously or not — whether this is still the right advisor. Also the natural moment for referral conversations and deepening the relationship.

Advisor
01Annual plan update
Full portfolio rebalance, tax review, updated IPS, compliance docs. Synthesis of a year's worth of notes, meetings, and drift into a fresh plan.
02Referral & growth
Ask for introductions at the right moment. Identify clients whose life stage just changed to warrant deeper advice.
03Practice review
Honest look at book health, revenue mix, capacity, and where next year's growth will come from.
Client
01Reassessment
Am I better off than last year? Is my plan still right for where my life is going? Rarely articulated — usually felt.
02Loyalty test
Subconsciously comparing the relationship to alternatives. A single clunky review can loosen a long-held trust.
03Forward pull
Wants to feel the plan is ambitious, not just preserving. Retirement isn't only about not running out — it's about what life looks like on the way there.
Intersection zone
Ambition gap
Client wants to grow — financially and in life. Advisor defaults to maintenance and risk management. Design opportunity: prompts and formats that make the annual review a forward-looking conversation, not a backwards-looking report.
